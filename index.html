The Book by Ding Zhao

Physical AI Safety

The Gateway to Deploying AI in the Real World

Artificial intelligence is leaving the screen. When a model errs in the physical world, the cost is not a poor recommendation but a damaged machine, a missed diagnosis, or a human life. This book develops, in one coherent treatment, the mathematical tools that treat uncertainty, distribution shift, constraint violation, and rare failure as first-class objects of study — closing the gap between what AI can demonstrate and what it can be trusted to do.

About the Author

Ding Zhao

Ding Zhao is an associate professor at Carnegie Mellon University, where he directs the Safe AI Lab. His research develops the mathematical foundations of trustworthy AI for physical systems — robustness, generalization, safe reinforcement learning, and rare-event certification — with applications in autonomous driving, robotics, and healthcare. This book grew out of his two decades of research, teaching, and industrial experience.

The Book

Closing the deployment gap

Self-driving cars, surgical assistants, warehouse fleets, home and factory robots: AI is moving from ranking and recommending into sensing, deciding, and acting. Yet there is a persistent gap between what AI can demonstrate and what it can be trusted to do. Closing it is not a matter of adding a safety checklist at the end of development — it requires mathematics that makes failure itself the object of study.

One framework, seven chapters

A physical AI system involves three mathematical objects: an environment model, a policy, and a human evaluation criterion. Each chapter studies a property of one object or an interaction of two — from robustness under bounded perturbation, through generalization under distribution shift and constraint safety, to certifying failure rates too rare to ever observe and the standards and regulation that authorize deployment.

A running case study

A hospital mobile manipulator threads through every chapter: the same robot that must be robust to a spoofed sensor in Chapter 2 must be certified against one-in-a-billion failures in Chapter 6, and enter a deployment dossier in Chapter 7.

Who it is for

Graduate students and practicing engineers who build, evaluate, or certify learning-based systems for physical deployment. The book is self-contained for a one-semester course, with tailored reading paths for machine-learning, robotics and control, and safety-and-regulation readers. Prerequisites are deliberately modest: graduate-level probability, linear algebra, and basic machine learning — Chapter 1 supplies the rest.

Exercises throughout

Every chapter ends with exercises tagged by type — derivation, conceptual, implementation, design — and by difficulty. All 68 have worked solutions; an instructor's solutions manual accompanies the course materials.

1 Mathematical Foundations 10 exercises

The mathematical toolkit behind every physical AI system, from deep networks and backpropagation to reinforcement learning and calibrated uncertainty.

Read this chapter online →

Defines physical AI — systems that sense, decide, and act in the physical world — and what makes its safety problem distinctive: irreversible actions, rare but consequential events, distribution drift, partial observability, and real-time decisions. Builds the supervised learning toolkit for perception, then the sequential decision-making toolkit from imitation learning to Markov Decision Processes and reinforcement learning, closing with uncertainty quantification and calibration.

2 Robustness 11 exercises

How adversaries attack physical AI, from imperceptible pixels to laser-spoofed LiDAR — and the certified defenses that hold the line under the worst case.

Read this chapter online →

Robustness is a system's ability to hold acceptable performance under worst-case disturbances within a stated budget. The chapter builds a taxonomy of robustness notions and threat models, covers attacks from digital and physically realizable evasion to poisoning and supply-chain compromise, then develops the defenses — adversarial training, randomized smoothing, deterministic verification, robust MDPs — all cast as a common maximin problem, ending with robustness in physical sensing.

3 Generalization 8 exercises

When the world shifts out from under a trained agent: measure how far it moved with optimal transport, and adapt with causality, meta-learning, and sim-to-real transfer.

Read this chapter online →

Generalization safety is the risk that an agent trained on one task distribution fails on another — a larger regime of shift than the bounded perturbations of robustness. The chapter formalizes tasks with the contextual MDP, introduces a four-way taxonomy of distribution shifts, uses optimal transport to measure task distance and bound target risk, and develops adaptation three ways: freeze-and-add continual learning, restrict-to-invariant causal features, and learn-to-adapt meta-learning — then applies it all to sim-to-real transfer and online task identification.

4 Constraint Safety 10 exercises

From CMDPs to control barrier functions — training policies that respect safety budgets, then guarding them at runtime so "safe on average" becomes "safe on this trajectory."

Read this chapter online →

Formulates safety as explicit constraints in sequential decision making via the constrained Markov Decision Process, and develops the methods that solve it online and from offline data: Lagrangian duality, trust regions, inference-based methods, and offline pessimism. The heart of the chapter is a deployment architecture that converts expectation-level training bounds into state-wise runtime safety via control-theoretic filters and run-time assurance — the learned policy proposes, the guard disposes.

5 Foundation Model Safety 9 exercises

When a generalist model drives a real actuator: dissecting vision-language-action failure modes and layering test environments, alignment, and runtime guardrails to contain them.

Read this chapter online →

Examines foundation models as physical controllers — vision-language-action models that map camera images and language instructions to motor commands — and their failure channels: perception hallucination, instruction misinterpretation, embodiment distribution shift, and adversarial scene injection. Builds a four-component vocabulary for generative world models used as test environments, develops the mathematics of alignment fine-tuning from RLHF to DPO and verifiable rewards, and closes with layered runtime guardrails, principled deference, and what red-teaming evidence actually establishes for a safety case.

6 Rare-Event Certification 11 exercises

Certifying safety for failures too rare to ever observe — from the rule of three to importance sampling and finite-sample bounds that turn statistical evidence into a safety case.

Read this chapter online →

Develops the statistical machinery for certifying systems whose failure probabilities are so small that naive testing would demand billions of trials. States the premises that fix what a certificate means, then builds estimators from naive Monte Carlo through importance sampling with provable efficiency to learning-based methods usable in high dimension — analyzing surrogate bias and one-sided bounds — and closes with the finite-sample certification verdict and the path from statistical evidence to safety standards.

7 Regulation, Standards, and Human Oversight 9 exercises

Where mathematical safety bounds meet the institutions — standards, regulators, and insurers — that decide whether a physical AI system is allowed to ship.

Read this chapter online →

Connects the technical evidence of the earlier chapters to the institutional process that authorizes deployment, in three movements: the standards and functional-safety spine, the human layer of oversight and shared autonomy, and the accountability stack of regulation, liability, and insurance. The safety case is the integrating artifact that consumes each prior chapter's evidence; the chapter closes with a one-page deployment dossier and open problems for the field.
